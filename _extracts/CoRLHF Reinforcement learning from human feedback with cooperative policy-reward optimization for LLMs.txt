a b s t r a c t
Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has succeeded remarkably in large language models (LLMs) 
alignment. However, in the standard LLMs RLHF method, a distribution mismatch exists between the genera-
tion policy and reward model (RM) when the policy continuously improves, but the RM remains unchanged. 
Moreover, the static RM may restrict the performance ceiling of the optimized policy. Furthermore, gather-
ing high-quality human preference datasets to train RM can be time-consuming and expensive. To solve the 
above limitations, we propose a reinforcement learning from human feedback with cooperative policy-reward 
optimization (CoRLHF) method to improve RLHF. CoRLHF optimizes the policy and RM iteratively and auto-
matically. In CoRLHF, the preference datasets of training RM are collected by the consensus scores of policy and 
RM on policy experiences, and the policy is trained by the iteratively improved RM. The iterative and automatic 
cooperative policy-RM optimization loop can improve the performance ceiling of the optimized policy. Further-
more, we show that CoRLHF is a promising method for weak-to-strong RLHF, and CoRLHF can be considered 
a bridge between RLHF and reinforcement learning from artiп¬Ғcial intelligence feedback. Experimental results 
show that CoRLHF outperforms RLHF in alignment with human preferences.

et al., 2020) demonstrated remarkable text generation capabilities, these

Recently, RLHF has garnered considerable attention (Bansal et al., 
2023; Ouyang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024b; Zhao et al., 2023). RLHF 
aims to train LLMs to align more closely with human preferences (Chris-
tiano et al., 2017). Ouyang et al. (2022) demonstrate that LLMs trained 
with RLHF exhibit greater creative and human alignment than mod-
els trained using supervised or self-supervised learning methods (Brown 
et al., 2020). Ouyang et al. (2022) also demonstrate that the importance 
of RLHF extends beyond enabling LLMs to adhere to human instruc-

instilling crucial attributes such as helpfulness, harmlessness, and hon-
esty. Due to these excellent traits, many works use RLHF to align LLMs 
(Bansal et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b, 2024b; Zhao et al., 2023). A de-
tailed review of LLMs RLHF is provided in Section 2.
However, RLHF in LLMs faces challenges. In the standard LLMs RLHF 
method, a distribution mismatch (Touvron et al., 2023) exists between 
the generation policy and reward model (RM) when the policy continu-
ously improves but the RM remains unchanged, although the standard 
RLHF method tries to use Kullback-Leibler divergence to ensure that 
the optimized policy is not too diп¬Җerent from the supervised п¬Ғne-tuning 
(SFT) policy. Moreover, the static RM may restrict the performance ceil-
ing of the optimized policy. Furthermore, gathering high-quality hu-
man preference datasets to train RM can be time-consuming and ex-
pensive. To solve the above limitations, we propose a reinforcement
learning from human feedback with cooperative policy-reward opti-
mization (CoRLHF) method to improve the RLHF method.

вҖў In this paper, we propose a reinforcement learning from human feed-

optimizes the policy and RM iteratively and automatically. The it-

вҖў We show that CoRLHF is a promising method for weak-to-strong 
RLHF. Furthermore, we show that CoRLHF can be considered a 
bridge between RLHF and RLAIF.
вҖў Experimental results show that CoRLHF outperforms RLHF in align-
ment with human preference.

2.  Related work
This section reviews alignment methods for LLMs, categorized into 
four classes: (1) RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017), (2) Implicit Reward 
(Rafailov et al., 2024), (3) RLAIF (Bai et al., 2022b), (4) and other meth-
ods (Casper et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 
2023b; Yao et al., 2023). First, we present a comprehensive discussion 
of RLHF methods, outlining their current research landscape. Second, 
we analyze the advancements and challenges in Implicit Reward and 
RLAIF methods. Third, we will introduce and discuss other alignment 
methods. Fourth, we concisely summarize the LLM alignment methods 
and their core ideas, advantages, and limitations. Finally, we discuss the 
works that are most related to our method and the diп¬Җerences between 
our method and those works.
RLHF and its variants. Wu et al. (2024b) propose the FINE-
GRAINED RLHF that trains on dense reward signals after each segment 
and incorporates multiple reward models for diп¬Җerent feedback types. 
Zhao et al. (2023) propose group preference optimization (GPO), align-
ing language models with the preferences of diп¬Җerent groups in a few-
shot manner. Sun et al. (2023) propose self-alignment with principle-
following reward models, which aligns base language models with 
minimal human supervision using a principle-following reward model 
trained on synthetic preference data. Wu et al. (2023b) use the pairwise 
proximal policy optimization algorithm to improve RLHF with relative 
feedback. Dai et al. (2023) propose Safe RLHF that explicitly decouples 
human preferences regarding helpfulness and harmlessness, avoiding 
the crowd workersвҖҷ confusion about the tension and allowing training 
separate reward and cost models. Lightman et al. (2023) show that pro-
cess supervision outperforms outcome supervision in training models for 
math dataset problems and propose process-supervised reward models. 
Yuan et al. (2023) introduce rank responses to align language models 
with human feedback (RRHF). RRHF aligns LLMs with human feedback 
by ranking responses based on the logarithm of conditional probabilities 
and optimizing them with ranking loss, serving as an extension of SFT 
and RM training while being simpler than the proximal policy optimiza-
tion (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). Gulcehre et al. (2023) propose Re-
inforced Self-Training (ReST), a method inspired by growing batch RL. 
ReST aligns LLMs with human preferences using oп¬„ine RL algorithms. 
Thus, ReST is more eп¬ғcient than online RLHF methods, as it generates 
a training dataset oп¬„ine for data reuse and avoids unstable techniques 
like PPO. Hu et al. (2023) present an oп¬„ine RLHF framework that aligns 
language models with human preferences using pre-generated samples 
and explores п¬Ғltering alignment, reward-weighted regression, and con-
ditional alignment methods.
Implicit Reward. Rafailov et al. (2024) propose a direct preference 
optimization (DPO) method that leverages a mapping between reward 
functions and optimal policies to show that the constrained reward max-
imization problem in RLHF can be optimized exactly with a single stage 
of policy training, essentially solving a classiп¬Ғcation problem on the 
human preference data. Among its variants, DPO-Positive (DPOP) (Pal 
et al., 2024) introduces a regularization term to prevent reward degra-
dation for preferred responses, particularly eп¬Җective for samples with 
small edit distances. However, it may struggle with more complex pref-
erence patterns. рқӣҪ-DPO (Wu et al., 2024a) dynamically adjusts its key pa-
rameter based on batch-level data quality, oп¬Җering enhanced robustness 
against noisy preferences but requiring careful calibration. IPO (Azar 
et al., 2024) addresses distribution shift through continuous online up-
dates, though determining optimal stopping points remains challenging. 
Token-level DPO (Zeng et al., 2024) extends the framework to granular 
token-wise optimization, enabling п¬Ғner control over generation quality 
at increased computational cost. While each variant addresses speciп¬Ғc 
limitations of standard DPO, their collective evolution points toward 
a future where hybrid approaches may combine dynamic adaptation 
with multi-granular optimization to achieve precise alignment and ro-
bust generalization across diverse tasks and domains.
RLAIF. Bai et al. (2022b) propose training an assistant LLM through 
self-improvement using a list of rules or principles instead of human-
labeled data to identify harmful outputs, known as вҖҷConstitutional AIвҖҷ 
or RLAIF. Bai et al. (2022a) use preference modeling and RLHF to п¬Ғne-
tune LLMs as helpful and harmless assistants, improving performance 
across various natural language processing tasks. They explore an it-
erated online training mode that updates preference models and RL 
policies weekly with fresh human feedback data, enhancing datasets 
and models. Cheng et al. (2023) propose an adversarial preference op-
timization (APO), where the LLM and the RM model update alterna-
tively via a min-max game. APO combined RLHF and generative ad-
versarial networks, and APO can be considered to apply the famous 
generative adversarial imitation learning (Ho & Ermon, 2016) to LLMs. 
Yuan et al. (2024) study self-rewarding language models, where the 
language model itself is used via LLM-as-a-Judge prompting to pro-
vide its own rewards during training. Lee et al. (2023b) highlight that 
RLAIF achieves comparable performances to RLHF according to human
evaluators.

4.  RLHF with cooperative policy-reward optimization
This section introduces our proposed CoRLHF framework. Sec-

4.2.  CoRLHF can be a weak-to-strong RLHF
An interesting contemporaneous work (Burns et al., 2024) studies the 
problem: Can weak model supervision elicit the full capabilities of a much 
stronger model? Burns et al. (2024) tested this using a range of pre-trained 
language models in the GPT-4 family on NLP, chess, and reward model-
ing tasks. Burns et al. (2024) found that when naively п¬Ғnetuning strong 
pre-trained models on labels generated by a weak model, they consis-
tently perform better than their weak supervisors, a phenomenon called 
weak-to-strong generalization. However, Burns et al. (2024) points out 
that they are still far from recovering the full capabilities of strong mod-
els with naive п¬Ғnetuning alone, suggesting that techniques like RLHF 
may scale poorly to superhuman models without further work.
The proposed CoRLHF is a promising method to solve the weak-
to-strong problem from an RL perspective. Note that CoRLHF diп¬Җers 
from Burns et al. (2024) because (Burns et al., 2024) proposes a super-
vised learning method to solve the weak-to-strong problem. However, 
CoRLHF solves this problem from an RL perspective. CoRLHF solves the 
weak-to-strong problem in two steps: We can train the weak model into 
an RM. The learned RM is called the Weak RM. Then, the Weak RM 
is used in the CoRLHF training process. After several iterative optimiza-
tion processes between policy and RM, the Weak RM is improved. Then, 
the CoRLHF can improve both the policy and RM. We veriп¬Ғed the eп¬ғ-
ciency of CoRLHF in Section 5. Thus, CoRLHF is a promising method to 
solve the weak-to-strong problem from an RL perspective, and CoRLHF 
can also be called weak-to-strong RLHF, shown in Fig. 2. Furthermore, 
Fig. 3 shows the relations of CoRLHF, RLHF, and RLAIF. CoRLHF can 
be considered a bridge between RLHF and RLAIF because Steps 1вҖ“3 in 
CoRLHF can be seen as RLHF, and Steps 4вҖ“6 in CoRLHF can be seen as 
RLAIF.

6.  Conclusion and future work
This paper proposed a reinforcement learning from human feed-
back with cooperative policy-reward optimization method. CoRLHF op-
timizes the policy and RM iteratively and automatically. The itera-
tive and automatic cooperative policy-RM optimization loop can im-
prove the performance ceiling of the optimized policy. Furthermore, 
we showed that CoRLHF is a promising method for weak-to-strong 
RLHF. Moreover, we showed that CoRLHF can be considered a bridge 
between RLHF and RLAIF. Experimental results showed that CoRLHF 
outperforms RLHF in alignment with human preference. We will apply 
the proposed method to LLMs with diп¬Җerent parameter sizes for future
work.
Limitation: Due to limitations in computational resources and re-
search budget, we were unable to experimentally evaluate the proposed 
method on LLMs beyond 14 billion parameters. Nevertheless, compre-
hensive validation was conducted on two architecturally distinct mod-
els - Qwen-14B (91.6k downloads on Hugging Face) and Platypus2-13B 
(1.18k downloads), which successfully veriп¬Ғed the generalizability of 
the proposed approach.
