A B S T R A C T
The integration of distributed energy resources (DERs) in virtual power plants (VPPs) is a critical enabler of low- 
carbon power system transformation; however, market uncertainty and conflicts among multiple decision- 
making agents pose major challenges for efficient scheduling. To address these issues, this paper proposes a 
hierarchical multi-agent reinforcement learning framework based on a hybrid-strategy sequential game. A novel 
dynamic role reduction mechanism is established for both supply and demand sides, enabling the aggregation of 
large-scale and heterogeneous VPP resources into a compact dual-agent sequential game representation. The 
framework employs a two-layer architecture. At the upper layer, hybrid pricing strategies are optimized through 
a sequential game where the supply side acts as the leader to maximize generation profits, and the demand side 
serves as the follower by adaptively adjusting load levels, achieving a robust subgame perfect Nash equilibrium 
despite conflicting objectives. In the lower layer, the scheduling task is formulated as a constrained Markov 
game, with Lagrangian relaxation incorporated to handle power balance, temporal coupling, and resource 
competition constraints, while quantifying both exogenous and endogenous uncertainties for adaptability in real- 
time market environments. We introduce the Sequential Game-based Multi-Agent Deep Deterministic Policy 
Gradient (SG-MADDPG) algorithm, which integrates the hierarchical game into an enhanced Multi-Agent Deep 
Deterministic Policy Gradient (MADDPG) framework. This algorithm features a graph neural network-enhanced 
adversarial dual-path critic to capture asymmetric supply–demand interactions and mitigate environmental non- 
stationarity, alongside a distributionally robust optimization (DRO) mechanism to improve training stability and 
policy robustness. Simulation results on a real-world VPP case demonstrate that the proposed approach increases 
overall profits by 14.7%-25.3%, reduces default risk by approximately 65%, and enhances deployable opera­
tional profits by more than 20%. Compared to centralized model predictive control (MPC) and traditional 
Stackelberg game-based methods, our framework exhibits superior performance, confirming its effectiveness and 
applicability in uncertain electricity market environments.
1. Introduction
1.1. Research Background and Significance
Under the global consensus of the “Dual Carbon” goals, the power 
system is transitioning from traditional fossil fuel dominance to clean 
energy. As an emerging energy aggregation model (Zhou et al., 2025; Lin 
et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025; Meng et al., 2025), the 
virtual power plant (VPP) integrates heterogeneous distributed energy 
resources (DERs)—such as distributed photovoltaic (PV), wind power, 
energy storage systems, and controllable loads—into a coordinated 
virtual entity through advanced information and communication 
technologies and control algorithms. This enables the unified dispatch 
and optimization of heterogeneous resources (Xu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 
2025) and has emerged as a key facilitator of the low-carbon transition 
(Liu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024; Kim and Kim, 2023).
Traditional VPP scheduling optimization methods emphasize math­
ematical modeling and solution efficiency, realizing power allocation, 
frequency regulation, and cost minimization through precise physical or 
economic models (Jendoubi and Bouffard, 2023; Zhang et al., 2021). For 
example, in Ref. (Guo et al., 2025) proposes a collaborative power 
control strategy for VPPs based on multi-agent systems that employs 
consensus algorithms to defend against cyberattacks, homomorphic 
encryption to protect privacy, and an event-triggered mechanism to

Game-theoretic methods focus on multi-agent interactions and 
equilibrium solutions, modeling strategic interactions among pro­
sumers, operators, and the grid to achieve Nash equilibrium or Stack­
elberg game equilibrium for optimizing resource allocation and 
efficiency of the markets. In Ref. (Alam et al., 2024) introduced a 
blockchain-based peer-to-peer energy trading framework, utilizing 
Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) to optimize prosumer profits 
and employing Ethereum smart contracts to achieve decentralized co­
ordination of DERs. Results indicate that this framework enhances grid 
stability and renewable energy utilization. However, the method does 
not adequately account for the dynamic process of sequential games and 
real-world market uncertainties. Ref. (Zhou et al., 2023) proposed a bi- 
level optimization model for an urban VPP, where the lower level con­
siders user satisfaction based on Incentive-Based Demand Response 
(IBDR), and the upper level optimizes generation dispatch and market 
transactions. Results show that the model supports supply–demand 
balance and minimizes costs. Nevertheless, it assumes a complete in­
formation game, thereby neglecting mixed strategies under uncertain 
environments. In (Yin and Ye, 2025) a distributed multi-objective Afri­
can Vulture Optimization Algorithm(AVOA), combined with deep neu­
ral networks to accelerate optimization. Tests on 118-bus and 1790-bus 
systems, this algorithm reduced carbon emissions by 3.5%-9.95% and 
costs by 2.16%-4.8%. Its limitation lies in the strong static assumptions 
and weak adaptability to dynamic uncertainties.
Reinforcement learning (RL) methods emphasize adaptation to un­
certainty and learning optimal policies through agent-environment in­
teractions to achieve robust scheduling in dynamic environments. In 
Ref. (H¨aberle et al., 2023) proposed hierarchical multi-agent deep 
reinforcement learning (HMADRL), which adopts a centralized training 
with decentralized execution (CTDE) paradigm to handle mutual deci­
sion exclusivity and introduces a hot experience pool to reduce explo­
ration. Simulations show the improved reliability of multi-energy multi- 
microgrid systems; however, the absence of game-equilibrium guidance 
leads to unstable policies in multi-agent, non-stationary environments. 
Ref. (Zhang et al., 2025) developed an active distribution network 
scheduling method based on a safe reinforcement learning framework to 
ensure compliance with distribution network constraints. Comparisons 
demonstrate superiority over existing safe reinforcement learning (SRL) 
and classical optimization methods; however, they neglect multi-agent 
game non-stationarity and fail to handle dynamic inter-agent conflicts 
effectively. In Ref. (Xu et al., 2024) proposed a robust preventive- 
corrective security-constrained Optimal Power Flow (OPF) model and 
addressed VPP faults using a Lagrangian-based soft actor (L-SAC) algo­
rithm. Tests on IEEE systems show improved computational efficiency 
and reliability; however, they do not consider sequential games under 
mixed strategies and struggle with multi-round interactions under 
uncertainty.
Recent advancements in Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) have 
further stimulated the practical application of machine learning 
methods in energy systems. In Ref. (Roy and Bhaduri, 2023) developed a 
Dense SPH-YOLOv5 model, which integrates a DenseNet module into 
the backbone network to enhance the retention and reuse of critical 
feature information. Additionally, extra feature fusion layers and a swin- 
Transformer prediction head (SPH) were incorporated, leveraging 
advanced self-attention mechanisms to reduce the algorithm's compu­
tational complexity. Ref. (Jiang et al., 2022) introduced a semi- 
supervised learning framework based on a multi-graph cooperative 
neural network, improving graph representation by integrating com­
plementary multi-graph information, achieving classification accuracy 
5–10% higher than baselines on multi-view datasets. The research on 
such advanced DRL models has demonstrated significant effectiveness 
for existing energy applications. In energy-specific applications, 
Ref. (Guo et al., 2025) proposed an evolutionary DRL algorithm, Cross- 
Entropy Method (CEM)-MADDPG, for electric vehicle thermal man­
agement, which enhances robustness through cross-entropy and reduces 
energy consumption by 19.8% compared to rule-based methods. Simi­
larly, Ref. (Li et al., 2025) applied the Deep Deterministic Policy 
Gradient (DDPG) algorithm from DRL for the multi-objective optimi­
zation of an integrated electricity-gas-heat energy system, improving the 
reward value by 6.09% over proximal policy optimization (PPO), 
thereby verifying reductions in cost and emissions. These DRL ad­
vancements motivate the integration of cutting-edge DL techni­
ques—such as attention mechanisms, graph cooperation, and 
evolutionary reinforcement—to enhance the practicality of machine 
learning in VPP optimization by addressing noisy data, multi-view 
representations, and multi-objective robustness.
To sum up, traditional optimization techniques are heavily biased 
towards model fidelity, game theoretical techniques are frequently 
biased towards restrictive static assumptions, and RL techniques are 
susceptible to non-stationary environments. Although recent DRL de­
velopments have made significant improvements in the feature extrac­
tion ability and robustness in aspects such as damage detection and 
energy system optimization, they have not been successfully extended to 
tackle sequential game dynamics in large-scale VPPs (Guan et al., 2025; 
Wei et al., 2025; Srivastava et al., 2024; Upadhayay et al., 2024; Xu Q, 
Su Z, Li P, et al. Cooperative Energy Provisioning Services with Virtual 
Power Plants in Smart Grid Internet of Things: A Coalition-Stackelberg 
Game Approach[J]. IEEE Internet of Things Journal, 2025). Existing 
methods tend to work well under deterministic scenarios, but show 
significant limitations when faced with uncertainty, heavy computa­
tional load and scalability issues, which constrain their use to large- 
scale, dynamic VPP scenarios. Moreover, these approaches have prob­
lems in solving collaborative conflicts between multiple agents (Zhang 
et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 
2023). Overall, most previous research takes a single methodological 
view and comprehensive frameworks that closely integrate game theo­
retic modeling with learning-based decision making are still lacking.
1.2. Research gaps and contributions
In summary, although there have been some significant de­
velopments in the literature, there are still some critical gaps. Existing 
studies often overlook the role of mixed-strategy sequential games in 
uncertain electricity market environments, where multi-agent ap­
proaches are often limited to pure strategies or synchronous decision- 
making. This limitation makes it impossible to accurately model dy­
namic interactions between supply-side leaders and demand-side fol­
lowers, leading to deviations from globally optimal solutions (Dai et al., 
2025; Chen et al., 2022; Golpîra and Marinescu, 2024; Liu et al., 2023; 
Shao et al., 2024). Also, most research assumes a fixed agent population 
or only considers single-level optimization and does not capture stra­
tegic interactions across multiple hierarchical levels. Multi-Agent RL 
(MARL) techniques also face serious challenges such as environmental 
non-stationarity and training instability (Zhang et al., 2025; Zhou and 
Preindl, 2023). Standard algorithms, such as Multi-Agent Deep Deter­
ministic Policy Gradient (MADDPG), can converge to local optima, and 
do not exploit the full benefits of Nash equilibrium guidance or repre­
sentational benefits of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Chen et al., 
2024; Wu et al., 2024).

To address these gaps, a bi-level optimization framework based on 
mixed-strategy sequential games is proposed within a MARL paradigm. 
The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
1) Hierarchical Hybrid-Strategy Framework for Large-Scale VPPs: 
This study addresses the shortcomings of traditional VPP scheduling 
methods in coordinating large-scale DERs and resolving multi-agent 
conflicts by proposing a hierarchical optimization framework groun­
ded on hybrid-strategy sequential games and MARL. A novel dynamic 
role-reduction mechanism is proposed to aggregate heterogeneous re­
sources into two agent types, the supply side and the load side. These 
agents interact non-cooperatively through the electricity price signals. 
At the upper level, the supply-side agents play the role of leaders to 
maximize profit through pricing strategies, and the load-side agents play 
the role of followers to dynamically adjust demand. This interaction 
leads the system to a subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium and takes into 
account multi-party conflicts. Unlike the traditional Stackelberg for­
mulations, the proposed approach eliminates assumptions of pure stra­
tegies and perfect information, which greatly enhances the scalability 
and adaptability to scenarios with tens of thousands of aggregated 
resources.
2) Constrained Markov Game and DRO for Lower-Level Scheduling: 
The lower-level scheduling is formulated as a constrained Markov game, 
with constraints such as power balance, temporal coupling, and resource 
competition handled via Lagrangian relaxation. Market disturbances 
and internal–external uncertainties are explicitly considered. By 
embedding this game into an improved MADDPG algorithm, we design a 
Nash equilibrium-oriented differentiated reward function, which guides 
multi-agent policies to converge to a Nash equilibrium while ensuring 
consistent expected returns across different pure strategies. This design 
effectively mitigates the poor policy convergence of traditional MARL in 
non-stationary environments.
3) Robustness to Non-Stationarity and Market Uncertainty: To 
further address environmental non-stationarity and market uncertainty, 
this study incorporates a Graph Neural Network-enhanced Implicit 
Adversarial Dual-path Critic (GIADC) structure at the upper level to 
implicitly model the asymmetric dynamic interactions between supply 
and demand, thereby decoupling the impact of environmental varia­
tions. At the lower level, a Distributionally Robust Optimization (DRO) 
mechanism is integrated to enhance robustness against model errors and 
volatile market conditions.
Through these innovations, the proposed framework demonstrates 
distinct advantages in coordinating heterogeneous resources, managing 
non-cooperative game interactions, and coping with market un­
certainties in VPPs. It effectively overcomes the limitations of traditional 
centralized scheduling, classical Stackelberg games, and basic MARL 
approaches in terms of scalability, multi-agent non-stationarity, and 
policy convergence, offering an efficient and reliable solution for 
optimal VPP scheduling in large-scale, dynamic market environments. 
Experimental results show that compared with centralized MPC opti­
mization and traditional Stackelberg benchmark, it improves global 
profit by 14.7%-25.3%, reduces default risk by 65%, and increases profit 
by more than 20% in actual deployment.

plant.” Through intelligent coordination and optimization, it achieves 
centralized control and scheduling of distributed resources, thereby

2.2. VPP scheduling framework
In summary, the VPP is an efficient and reliable energy ecosystem 
constructed using digital means. The high heterogeneity of internal re­
sources poses severe challenges for operational scheduling. According to 
the supply–demand characteristics of VPP resources across different 
periods, these heterogeneous resources are aggregated into two primary 
roles—the “supply side” and the “load side”, which greatly reduces the 
complexity of the scheduling model.
Within this framework, DERs are abstracted into one virtual entity, 
mainly to reduce the dimensionality of the strategy space and enhance 
the scalability. This formulation implicitly assumes that supply side 
DERs constitute a cooperative coalition coordinated by the VPP oper­
ator, which is in line with common operational paradigms in current 
VPP implementations (Li et al., 2025). Moreover, in liberalized elec­
tricity markets where supply-side DERs may be owned by independent 
entities with competing interests, the model can be naturally extended 
to include internal competition. Specifically, individual DERs or clusters

A bi-level RL framework was adopted, which divides the overall 
scheduling problem into two layers: “high-level decision-making” and 
“low-level optimization.” The constructed hierarchical reinforcement 
learning framework consists of an upper-level global coordination layer 
and a lower-level local execution layer. The upper level comprises a 
supply-side controller and a load-side controller. The former is respon­
sible for formulating the overall electricity price and output allocation 
strategy in line with the system's economic objectives and sup­
ply–demand balance, whereas the latter plans the global response 
scheme based on price signals and load forecasts. Together, they con­
structed a price–load collaborative global scheduling strategy to guide 
the specific decisions of the lower level. The lower level consists of 
multiple subordinate units on the supply side and load side, which 
interact in real time through price signals and electric energy. The 
upper-level game captures the slower trends of strategic coordination 
under uncertainty, while the lower-level agents operate at a finer tem­
poral granularity, continuously adjusting their actions in response to 
evolving system states. The overall framework is illustrated in Fig. 1.
3. Modeling of upper-level dynamic sequential game theory
Based on the division of roles into the supply side and the demand 
side, the supply-side pricing strategy directly influences the demand- 
side consumption behavior, whereas the demand-side response affects 
the supply-side revenue. Therefore, the supply side should play a leading 
role by participating in the VPP's optimization scheduling through a 
dynamic pricing mechanism. This mechanism guides the demand side to 
shift consumption patterns and alleviates grid congestion pressures. In 
response, the demand side dynamically adjusts its consumption 
schedule based on price signals to reduce costs and obtain demand 
response incentives, as shown in Fig. 2.

5.3. Experimental conclusions
Experimental results show that the proposed bi-level MARL frame­

centralized MPC, conventional Stackelberg game, standard MADDPG 
and DECI-VPP under uncertain market conditions. In benchmark sce­
narios, the framework significantly boosts the profit at the global level, 
showing significant improvements with respect to both MPC and 
Stackelberg game approaches. It also has faster convergence and 
significantly less deviations from the Nash equilibrium. Through 
parameter optimization, performance is further improved and the 
number of iterations needed for convergence is noticeably reduced. 
Ablation studies confirm that the DRO, GNN enhancement and Nash 
equilibrium constraint are critical to improving the robustness, 
modeling inter-agent interactions and guiding the equilibrium conver­
gence, respectively. Under high uncertainty scenarios, the proposed 
framework exhibits a high robustness, indicated by narrower ranges of 
profit fluctuations and much lower DR than those of the Stackelberg 
game and the standard MADDPG approaches. Real-world case studies in 
China demonstrate significant profit improvements and a lot of risk 
reduction. Comparisons with international VPP projects also provide 
further validation of the generalisability of the framework, showing the 
clear growth in profits and effective control of risks. Overall, the pro­
posed framework can effectively coordinate the heterogeneous re­
sources, avoid the imbalance penalty, and support the low-carbon 
transition of power systems in accordance with the “dual-carbon” goals, 
which shows its good engineering application value. All the experiments 
were implemented with PyTorch 2.0, and the full code is publicly 
available at: https://github.com/IAMHUT/Hierarchical-Optimization-

6. Summary and Outlook
This work tackles the issues of heterogeneous resource scheduling 
and multi-stakeholder conflict coordination in VPPs under uncertain 
market conditions. It proposes a two-layer MARL scheduling framework 
based on a mixed strategy sequential game. Using a dynamic role 
reduction mechanism, the framework constructs a sequential game in a 
mixed electricity price strategy space, which achieves non-cooperative 
coupling between supply and demand sides. The framework uses a 
two-layer architecture: the upper layer optimizes mixed pricing strate­
gies to dynamically balance conflicting interests and the lower layer 
formulates the scheduling problem as a constrained Markov game. 
Lagrangian relaxation is added to address power balance, temporal 
coupling and resource competition constraints and exogenous and 
endogenous uncertainties are explicitly quantified to ensure adapt­
ability in real-time markets. Furthermore, the proposed SG-MADDPG 
algorithm incorporates GNN enhanced adversarial dual-path critic to 
address the environmental non-stationarity, DRO, curriculum learning 
and risk-sensitive reward shaping to improve training stability and 
operational robustness. Experimental results show that the online 
inference per time step consists of forward propagation through

Theorem E1: Under standard assumptions (such as the concavity of the payoff function and the compactness of the policy space), the DROMARL

problem, and its solution exists and is unique. Second, it is proven that the iterative update process of the entire policy profile constitutes a quasi- 
monotonic variational inequality. Using Rosen's (1965) classic conclusion on the existence of equilibrium points in concave games, it can be 
proven that the algorithm can converge to an equilibrium point.

attention, where the query-key vector dimension per head is 64. The node features are initialized with the observation dimension and aggregated via 
GAT into a hidden representation hm. Subsequently, an MLP generates the final embedding for either the supply-side or load-side. The Critic outputs a 
scalar Q-value through a linear layer. The total parameter count for the entire GIADC ranges approximately from 50,000 to 60,000.
The time complexity of the proposed method is primarily dominated by the GAT operations and Critic updates. For N agents, the per-step 
complexity of GAT aggregation is O=N2 • d, where d denotes the feature dimension; in practice, N remains small and manageable. The overall 
training complexity is O = T • B • (N • d2)), with T representing the number of iterations and B the batch size. The space complexity involves 
approximately 100,000 parameters, requiring around 500 MB of memory usage.
